◈ Sprint VLamax Test

VLamax — sprint test

Enter your pre/post-sprint blood lactate values and we'll compute your maximum glycolytic capacity using Mader's 1994 protocol — corrected for the alactic phosphagen contribution. Save the result to your profile; the full Lactate Step Test requires it.

Sprint inputs

Protocol: warm up at least 10 minutes, take a pre-sprint capillary lactate, perform an all-out 15-second sprint (cycling: ergometer at max effort; running: maximal track or treadmill sprint), then sample peak lactate at 3, 5, 7, and 9 minutes post — take the highest reading as La_peak.

Advanced — phosphagen time
What is VLamax, and why does it matter?

VLamax is the maximum rate at which your body can accumulate lactate in the blood — a proxy for the glycolytic flux ceiling of your muscle. It's expressed in mmol/L per second.

A higher VLamax is characteristic of sprint/power-trained athletes. A lower VLamax is characteristic of long-duration aerobic athletes — and in fact, lowering VLamax through aerobic-base training is one of the few well-established ways to raise your MLSS (anaerobic threshold) at a fixed VO₂max.

VLamax and VO₂max together are the two primary inputs to the Mader bioenergetic model. Together they determine MLSS, fat oxidation rates, training zone boundaries, and substrate crossover points.

Why subtract the phosphagen time?

The first ~3 seconds of a maximal sprint are powered primarily by ATP and phosphocreatine — not by glycolysis. The standard Mader formula corrects for this by subtracting the alactic phosphagen time (default 3.5 s, range 2.5–4.5 s) from the sprint duration when computing glycolytic rate.

Without the correction, VLamax would be systematically underestimated, especially for short (10–15s) sprints where the phosphagen fraction is large.
